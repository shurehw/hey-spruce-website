What is a Grease Trap and How Does It Work?

A grease trap (also called a grease interceptor) is a plumbing device designed to intercept most greases and solids before they enter the wastewater disposal system. In Los Angeles, these devices are mandatory for all commercial food preparation facilities.

How Grease Traps Function

Understanding the mechanics helps you maintain your system properly:

  1. Wastewater Entry: Greasy water flows from sinks, dishwashers, and prep areas into the trap
  2. Temperature Reduction: Water cools down, causing fats, oils, and grease (FOG) to separate
  3. Separation Process: Grease floats to the top, solids sink to the bottom, cleaner water remains in middle
  4. Water Exit: Only the middle layer of relatively clean water flows into the sewer system
  5. Retention: FOG and solids are trapped until professional pumping removes them

Why LA Requires Grease Traps

Critical Reasons for Grease Trap Requirements:

  • Prevent Sewer Blockages: FOG causes 47% of all sewer overflows in LA
  • Protect Infrastructure: LA County maintains 6,700+ miles of sewers
  • Environmental Protection: Prevents contamination of Santa Monica Bay and Pacific Ocean
  • Public Health: Reduces sanitary sewer overflows (SSOs) that pose health risks
  • Cost Savings: Annual sewer maintenance costs LA County $28 million

How Often to Clean Your Grease Trap in Los Angeles

The 25% Rule

LA County requires grease trap cleaning when FOG and solids occupy 25% of the total capacity. This is the legal standard, but practical frequency depends on several factors:

Restaurant Type Volume Level Trap Size Recommended Frequency
Fine Dining High-fat menu items 1,000-2,000 gal Every 30 days
Full-Service Restaurant Moderate cooking 1,000-1,500 gal Every 45-60 days
Fast Casual High volume, fried foods 750-1,000 gal Every 30-45 days
Pizza Restaurant Moderate grease 500-1,000 gal Every 60 days
Coffee Shop/Bakery Low grease 250-500 gal Every 90 days
24-Hour Diner Continuous operation 1,500-2,000 gal Every 21-30 days
Fried Chicken/Wings Very high grease 1,000-1,500 gal Every 14-21 days

Factors That Increase Cleaning Frequency

  • Menu Type: Deep-fried foods generate 3-5x more grease
  • Operating Hours: 24-hour operations need more frequent service
  • Volume: High-volume restaurants (500+ meals/day) need monthly service minimum
  • Trap Size: Undersized traps require more frequent pumping
  • Poor Practices: Lack of pre-scraping increases solid buildup
  • Multiple Locations: Shared traps require more frequent attention
  • Seasonal Variations: Summer heat accelerates FOG accumulation

Creating Your Cleaning Schedule

Calculate Your Required Frequency:

  1. Measure Initial Accumulation: Check FOG level after 2 weeks of operation
  2. Calculate Rate: If 10% full after 2 weeks, you reach 25% in 5 weeks
  3. Build in Safety Margin: Subtract 1-2 weeks for safety
  4. Set Recurring Service: Schedule slightly more frequently than calculated

Example: If your 1,000-gallon trap reaches 25% capacity in 50 days, schedule cleaning every 40 days.

The Professional Grease Trap Cleaning Process

What to Expect During Service

Understanding the process helps ensure you get quality service:

  1. Step 1: Pre-Service Preparation (5-10 minutes)

    • Technician locates trap and removes cover/lid
    • Visual inspection of trap condition
    • Measurement of FOG and solids levels
    • Photos taken for documentation

  2. Step 2: Initial Pumping (15-30 minutes)

    • Vacuum truck hose inserted into trap
    • Complete removal of all liquid, FOG, and solids
    • Multiple pumping points used for thorough extraction
    • Volume measured and recorded

  3. Step 3: Interior Cleaning (10-20 minutes)

    • Power washing of interior walls
    • Scraping of hardened grease deposits
    • Cleaning of baffles and internal components
    • Final rinse and inspection

  4. Step 4: Component Inspection (5-10 minutes)

    • Check gaskets and seals for wear
    • Inspect baffles for damage
    • Verify inlet and outlet pipes are clear
    • Note any needed repairs

  5. Step 5: Refilling and Testing (5-10 minutes)

    • Refill trap with clean water to proper level
    • Run water through system to verify flow
    • Check for leaks
    • Secure lid properly

  6. Step 6: Documentation (5 minutes)

    • Complete service manifest with date, volume, condition
    • Issue compliance certificate
    • Provide copy for your records
    • Update digital tracking system (if applicable)

Total Time: 45-90 minutes depending on trap size and condition

Emergency Grease Trap Situations

If you experience a grease trap overflow or backup:

  1. Immediate Actions (First 15 minutes)
    • Stop using all sinks and dishwashers
    • Close affected area to customers
    • Place warning signs
    • Call emergency grease trap service
    • Notify manager/owner
  2. Containment (Next 30 minutes)
    • Place absorbent materials around spill
    • Prevent contamination of food areas
    • Document with photos
    • Note time and extent of backup
  3. Professional Response (Within 2-4 hours)
    • Emergency pumping service arrives
    • Complete trap cleaning and inspection
    • Identify cause of failure
    • Clear any line blockages
  4. Cleanup and Sanitization
    • Professional cleaning of affected areas
    • Complete sanitization
    • Verification testing
    • Documentation for health department
  5. Follow-Up (Next 24-48 hours)
    • Determine root cause
    • Implement corrective actions
    • Update cleaning schedule if needed
    • Report to health department (if required)
    • Review insurance coverage

When to Report to LA County

You MUST report to the health department if:

  • Overflow reaches food preparation areas
  • Sewage backs up into customer areas
  • Overflow exits your property
  • Any possibility of environmental contamination
  • Closure necessary for cleanup

Failure to report can result in additional fines and penalties.
